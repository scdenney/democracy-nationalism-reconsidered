# Democracy doesn't restrain nationalism. A civic idea of the nation does.

We tend to assume that democratic values and nationalism pull in opposite directions. Democracy is supposed to disperse power and protect minorities. Nationalism is supposed to draw hard lines around who belongs. So people who care about democracy should, the thinking goes, be less nationalistic. The assumption is everywhere, and right now it carries a lot of weight, because nationalism has become shorthand for nativism and the populist right.

It turns out the assumption is half right, and the half it gets wrong is the interesting half.

Using the 2023 ISSP National Identity survey, which asked the same battery in 29 countries, I looked at how people's commitment to democratic *rights* relates to several different kinds of nationalism. The first thing you have to do is stop treating "nationalism" as one thing. Being proud of your country is not the same as wanting to keep immigrants out, and neither is the same as believing your nation is superior. The survey lets you separate them.

Once you do, the Western pattern is clean. People who say that minority rights, participation, and the right to dissent matter for democracy are meaningfully *less* likely to hold exclusionary views. They are less hostile to immigrants and less wedded to a bloodline-and-birth definition of who is "truly" one of us, and no readier to call their country better than the rest. And they are no less *proud* of their country than anyone else. What democratic commitment pushes against is exclusion. It leaves attachment alone. That is the reassuring part, and it is real.

Here is the part that should give us pause.

This relationship is not a property of democratic values. It is a property of a particular *idea of the nation*. And where that idea is different, the relationship falls apart.

Look at East Asia. South Korea and Taiwan are, by the standard institutional measures, every bit as liberal-democratic as Western Europe. V-Dem scores them right up there with Germany and the Netherlands. If "more liberal democracy means less exclusionary nationalism" were a law, it should hold there. It doesn't. In Korea and Taiwan, the people who most value democratic rights are *not* less exclusionary, and if anything they are *more* attached to an ethnic, ancestry-based idea of the nation, and prouder of it.

So what separates the West from East Asia here? Not how democratic the institutions are. The thing that predicts whether democratic commitment restrains exclusion is whether the nation is imagined in *civic* terms (membership is about shared institutions and participation) or in *ethnic* terms (membership is about ancestry, birth, and blood). When you put both candidates head to head — the civic-versus-ethnic content of nationhood and the institutional liberalism of the regime — the content of nationhood wins, decisively, and institutional liberalism adds essentially nothing once you account for it.

You can see it in one picture. Sort countries from the most civic idea of the nation to the most ethnic. Canada, Sweden, the United States, and Germany sit at the civic end. The Philippines, South Africa, Russia, and India sit at the ethnic end. Korea and Taiwan are well toward the ethnic end despite their liberal institutions. Now plot how strongly democratic-rights commitment restrains anti-immigrant hostility in each country. The restraining effect is strong at the civic end and gone (even slightly reversed) at the ethnic end. The line is clear. The same plot against institutional liberalism is a scatter of noise.

Why would Korea and Taiwan look this way? Because of how they became democracies. In the West, the civic-national idea grew up partly *against* ethnic exclusion. In Korea and Taiwan, democratization came *through* national mobilization, not against it. A genealogical, ethnic sense of the nation was carried into the democratic era rather than dissolved by it. So there, valuing democratic rights and feeling the pull of the ethnic nation are not in tension. They are part of the same package. This also reframes a long-running puzzle about China, where pro-democracy citizens are reliably *more* nationalistic. People usually chalk that up to life under an authoritarian regime. But if it shows up in consolidated East Asian democracies too, the regime isn't the explanation. The idea of the nation is.

There's a second wrinkle worth flagging, because it's easy to confuse the two things the survey measures. Valuing democratic *rights* is one thing. Thinking democracy is *working well* is another. They behave completely differently. People who think their democracy is performing well are markedly *prouder* of their country, and even more likely to say it's superior. Satisfaction with how democracy is going feeds national pride. Principled commitment to democratic rights does not. Two faces of "democratic attitudes," pointing in different directions.

A note on what this is and isn't. It's cross-sectional and correlational, so I'm not claiming that teaching people to value democratic rights would cause them to drop their prejudices. And the survey scales don't mean exactly the same thing in every country. Comparing the raw *levels* of nationalism across 29 very different societies is a mug's game, which is why I compare *relationships* within each context rather than levels across them. (The fact that these scales aren't fully comparable across borders is itself part of the story. The nation means different things in different places.)

But the bottom line is solid, and it's worth saying plainly given the moment we're in. If you're hoping that deepening citizens' commitment to democracy will, on its own, hold nativism in check, the evidence offers cold comfort. That works only where the nation is already understood as a civic community. Where the nation is understood as a community of descent, democratic commitment won't do that work, no matter how liberal the institutions are. What actually restrains exclusion is a civic idea of who counts as one of us, and that idea is a feature of political culture that no constitution can guarantee.
